    5. [Reading Question: CSM Exercise 1.8.2] In his May 17, 2010 op-ed column in The New York Times Bob Herbert noted that the dropout rate for American high school students was one every 26 seconds. [R10] Is this number reasonable?
    • Let‚Äôs estimate.
    • First, how many American high school students are there? We‚Äôll assume this corresponds with the proportion of Americans between ages \(14\) and \(18\text{,}\) inclusive. This is probably between \(5\) and \(15\%\) of Americans.
    • We use a geometric mean to get a single estimate:
      \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} 5\% & =5\times10^{0}\%\\ 15\% & =1.5\times10^{1}\%\\ \text{geometric mean} & =\frac{1.5+5}{2}\times10^{0.5}=3.25\times10^{0.5}\approx10\%. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    • around \(10\%\) (guess) of Americans, and there are about \(300\) million Americans, so we estimate that there are
      \begin{equation*} .1\times300,000,000=30,000,000 \end{equation*}
      American high-school students each year. Our answer here is ‚Äútens of millions‚Äù, which fits with the actual number of about \(56.6\) million. [Note that we don‚Äôt round; we just count the number of zeroes, which here is \(7\text{,}\) meaning our answer is ‚Äútens of millions‚Äù.]
    • Assume that \(20\%\) (perhaps a high estimate) of American high-school students drop out each year. That‚Äôs
      \begin{equation*} .2(30,000,000)=6,000,000 \end{equation*}
      dropouts per year.
    • We convert to dropouts per second:
      \begin{equation*} \frac{6,000,000\text{ dropouts}}{\text{year}}\times\frac{1\text{ year}}{365\text{ days}}\times\frac{1\text{ day}}{24\text{ hours}}\times\frac{1\text{ hour}}{60\text{ min}}\times\frac{1\text{ min}}{60\text{ sec}}=0.19\approx\frac{0.2\text{ dropouts}}{\text{second}}. \end{equation*}
    • Finally, we compute
      \begin{equation*} \frac{0.2\text{ dropouts}}{\text{second}}\times\frac{26\text{ seconds}}{1\text{ 26-second period}}=\frac{5.2\text{ dropouts}}{\text{26-second period}} \end{equation*}
      which is higher (rounded up) than Herbert‚Äôs estimate. However, we note that we purposefully overestimated the dropout rate in order to be generous to Herbert‚Äôs estimate, so we could easily be off by a factor of \(5\) (\(1\) vs. \(5.2\) dropouts per \(26\) seconds).

    Subsection 9.1.5 Fermi Estimation

    • In order to verify claims made in the media, to compute how much sea level rise would be associated with a \(1^{\circ}\) rise in global sea temperature, or to test whether the claims Trump makes in his next State of the Union address are correct, it‚Äôs essential to have an ability to estimateto lie (or oversimplify) skillfully and in a way that lets us better understand the truth.
    • I make many mistakes on this type of problem until I think it through carefully!]
    • Tools for ‚Äúback-of-the-envelope calculations‚Äù aka Fermi estimation
      • Estimationround up or down to make calculations easier and numbers less messy. As we saw when estimating the number of classrooms needed for the US household wealth, rounding before the computation ends can introduce error. Therefore, if you have a calculator, it‚Äôs best to plug into the calculator before rounding, whereas if you‚Äôre computing by hand, rounding may be necessary in order to increase the accuracy of your computations. Everyone makes arithmetic mistakesyou‚Äôll see me do it plenty of times!
      • Dimensional analysis: start with the given and multiply by fractions, canceling out the units, until you get something in the units your answer should be in. This is almost always your answer!
      • Only guess up to an order of magnitude, i.e. the number of zeroes.
    • Why bother with Fermi estimates, if your estimates are likely to be off by a factor of 2 or even 10? Often, getting an estimate within a factor of 10 or 20 is enough to make a decision. So Fermi estimates can save you a lot of time, especially as you gain more practice at making them.
    6. Can we say for sure that there were less than \(300\) million internatinoal phone calls per day in \(2007\text{?}\)
    • Nope! We only can be accurate up to, at most, an order of magnitudethat is, there were probably millions but not billions of international phone calls made by Americans per day in \(2007\text{.}\)
    • We can say our estimate is \(3\times10^{8}\) or \(300,000,000\text{,}\) but the only part we‚Äôre fairly sure about is the eight zeroes, the \(10^{8}\text{.}\)

    • Tips for estimation:
      • Change the rules to make arithmetic easier. There weren‚Äôt exactly \(300\) million Americans in \(2007\)the actual population was something like \(301.2\) million. But since we‚Äôre just giving an estimate anyway, we don‚Äôt care about the \(1.2\text{.}\) We round to the nearest hundred million!
        • For this reason, we only care about how many zeroes your answer has, not the actual number. So for our purposes \(1,000,000\) and \(9,000,000\) are the same because they give us the answer of ‚Äúmillions‚Äù.
      • Writing all those zeroes can get unwieldy, so we use scientific notation: \(1\) with \(14\) zeroes after it is the same as \(1\times10^{14}\text{.}\) Nine with seventy zeroes after it is written \(9\times10^{70}\text{.}\)
      • Divide and conquer: split the problem up by first figuring out all the things we need to know, then using those things in some order to get an answer.
      • Use ranges. If you feel fairly confident that there are between \(1000\) and \(300000\) Americans who don‚Äôt like Harry Potter (between \(1\times10^{3}\) and \(3\times10^{5}\) in scientific notation), average the exponents and the single-digit numbers to get your estimate. Here we‚Äôd average \(1\) and \(3\) to get \(2\) and \(3\) and \(5\) to get \(4\text{,}\) so your answer would be \(2\times10^{4}=20,000\) Americans.
        • This is called taking a geometric mean, which is easier to take than a ‚Äúregular‚Äù average when dealing with big numbers and exponents.
      • Look up related data, if it‚Äôs available, to check the reasonableness of your estimation. For example, when estimating the number of active COVID-19 cases in SLC, you could look up scientific estimates of the proportion of COVID-19 cases that are reported, multiply that proportion by the population of SLC, and see if it‚Äôs in the same ballpark (same number of zeroes) as your estimate.

        \begin{equation*} 10\times1,000,000=10,000,000 \end{equation*}
        Americans arrested.
      2. We now estimate seconds/year:
        \begin{equation*} 1\text{ year}\times\frac{350\text{ days}}{\text{year}}\times\frac{25\text{ hours}}{\text{day}}\times\frac{60\text{ min}}{\text{hr}}\times\frac{60\text{ sec}}{\text{min}}=30,000,000\text{ sec}. \end{equation*}
      3. This means that there were
        \begin{equation*} \frac{10,000,000}{30,000,000}\approx0.3\text{ arrests/sec} \end{equation*}
        or about one arrest every \(3\) sec.
      4. Now note that multiple people could be swept up in a single pot bust. Assume \(2\) people on average per bust, that gives
        \begin{equation*} 0.3\frac{\text{arrests}}{\text{sec}}\times\frac{1\text{ bust}}{2\text{ arrests}}=0.15\frac{\text{busts}}{\text{sec}} \end{equation*}
        or \(15\) busts every \(100\) sec. This is on the same order of magnitude as the ACLU‚Äôs estimate, so their estimate is reasonable.
